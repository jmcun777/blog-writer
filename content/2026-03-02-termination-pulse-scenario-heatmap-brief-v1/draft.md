# In China Employee Termination: Pulse Scenario-Heatmap Brief for Foreign Employers (2026 Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- A **scenario heatmap** helps foreign employers focus on the highest-risk termination situations first.
- Legal route clarity is the entry point, but each scenario must also pass evidence, communication, and settlement checks.
- Medium/high-heat scenarios often fail due to execution drift, not legal ignorance.
- Payroll and IIT handling should be embedded in every scenario review.
- Heatmap-led planning improves speed and decision quality for non-China stakeholders.

## Pulse lead
Termination risk in China is not evenly distributed. Some scenarios are low-friction and predictable; others are high-friction and quickly become dispute-prone if one control slips. A heatmap approach makes this visible early, so teams allocate attention where it matters most.

This run uses a **scenario-intensity format** to help foreign clients identify where to slow down, escalate, or reroute.

## Plain-language legal/regulatory context first
China’s Labor Contract Law defines route boundaries for employer-initiated termination. Implementation regulations and SPC interpretation shape practical review expectations, especially around fairness, process reliability, and documentation quality. For foreign employers, this means route legality must be paired with execution coherence to hold up in practice.

## Scenario heatmap (low / medium / high)
### Scenario 1 (Low heat): Mutual separation with complete records
- **Risk profile:** lower volatility if terms and documentation are aligned.
- **Critical checks:** route memo consistency, settlement lock, signed delivery records.
- **Failure trigger:** unclear payment/tax explanation post-agreement.

### Scenario 2 (Medium heat): Performance-based unilateral attempt with partial records
- **Risk profile:** elevated if evidence chronology is weak.
- **Critical checks:** objective standards, communication history, manager language discipline.
- **Failure trigger:** route narrative mismatch between HR/legal/manager.

### Scenario 3 (High heat): Restructuring-linked exits across cities
- **Risk profile:** high due to multi-party coordination and local variance.
- **Critical checks:** selection logic documentation, city-sensitive escalation, payout/tax consistency.
- **Failure trigger:** inconsistent local execution under global timeline pressure.

### Scenario 4 (High heat): Refusal-to-sign plus rapid escalation threat
- **Risk profile:** high procedural sensitivity.
- **Critical checks:** refusal protocol, witness/service method, legal escalation path.
- **Failure trigger:** ad hoc meeting responses and incomplete service logs.

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- Heatmaps help teams spend governance effort where dispute probability is highest.
- HQ-local alignment improves when risk is scenario-labeled instead of abstract.
- Leadership can sequence actions: low-heat execute, medium-heat stabilize, high-heat escalate.
- City-level uncertainty can be weighted per scenario without changing global policy.

For cross-border operators, this model reduces “one-template-for-all” mistakes and supports better resource planning for legal, payroll, and manager readiness. It also improves executive communication, because escalation requests can be tied to a specific heat signal and a concrete remediation plan instead of broad qualitative concern.

### Cross-border operating notes
- **Time-zone lag:** define delegated approvers for high-heat cases to avoid rushed local decisions.
- **Language control:** assign one controlling legal-language draft and one validated operational translation.
- **Vendor context:** ensure payroll vendors receive route context and exception flags, not just payout lines.
- **Manager alignment:** require a short pre-meeting alignment checkpoint in medium/high heat cases.

## Daily operations SOP
1. Tag each case into a heatmap scenario.
2. Confirm route legality and score scenario heat.
3. Assign scenario-specific control owners and closure deadlines.
4. Lock settlement/IIT before communication release in medium/high heat cases.
5. Run pre-execution heat re-check after any new fact.
6. Execute with scenario log and complete post-case audit.

## Required document checklist
- Scenario tag and heat rationale note
- Route memo + legal signoff
- Evidence index + chronology check
- Communication package + language control note
- Settlement worksheet + IIT method + version lock
- Exception protocol + override approval trail
- Service/payment proof
- Final closure audit + archive manifest

## Exception handling playbook
- **Heat increases pre-execution:** pause and apply next-level controls.
- **New facts alter route fit:** reopen route and scenario heat scoring.
- **Settlement uncertainty appears late:** hold outward communication until relock.
- **Override request in high heat:** written risk acceptance required.

## System implementation notes
Add heatmap fields to HRIS/payroll:
- scenario id + heat score
- owner + closure SLA
- route fit flag and revision history
- settlement lock + IIT fields
- override trail
- final scenario outcome score

### Minimum data model
- Case: entity, city, scenario tag, route type.
- Heat log: score, prior score, reason, changed-by.
- Action log: owner, due date, evidence link.
- Override log: approver, rationale, validity period.
- Closure log: final heat score, unresolved items, archive confirmation.

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** treating all cases as equal risk.  
  **Prevention:** mandatory scenario heat tagging.
- **Mistake:** under-scoring settlement risk in medium/high heat cases.  
  **Prevention:** settlement lock as hard precondition.
- **Mistake:** no heat re-check after new facts.  
  **Prevention:** auto-triggered re-scoring rule.

## What to do now

3. Make settlement and route checks non-waivable in medium/high heat.
4. Add city-specific heat modifiers.
5. Train managers on high-heat communication controls.

## Heatmap KPI dashboard
Track: case count by heat level, average closure time by heat tier, override rate in high heat, and post-action incidents linked to medium/high heat cases. Add prevention KPI: reroute rate after heat re-scoring.

For leadership, use two dashboard views:
- **Weekly execution view:** open medium/high heat cases, unresolved owner actions, and SLA breaches.
- **Monthly governance view:** repeat high-heat triggers, city-level variance trends, and override quality.

## Rollout recommendation
- **Week 1-2:** pilot heat scoring on medium-risk cases and calibrate scenario definitions.
- **Week 3-4:** include high-risk cases and enforce settlement lock as non-waivable in medium/high heat tiers.
- **Month 2+:** integrate heatmap metrics into leadership risk review and local counsel calibration.

Phased rollout improves scoring consistency and prevents noise-driven escalations in early adoption.

## What to watch next

- Ongoing city-level variation in practical review standards.

## FAQ
### Is heatmap scoring legally required?
No. It is an execution-governance framework.

### Can small teams use this model?
Yes, with simplified heat tiers and clear ownership.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral, restructuring, refusal, protected-status, and multi-city complex cases.
