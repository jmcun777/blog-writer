# China Employee Termination 2026: Pulse Casefile Scorecard Brief for Foreign Employers

## Key takeaways
- In China, termination success is often determined by **casefile quality**, not just business intent.
- A scorecard approach helps foreign employers quantify readiness before taking irreversible communication steps.
- The three highest-impact controls are route legality, evidence consistency, and payroll/tax closure readiness.
- City-level validation should be treated as a required score component, not discretionary input.
- Legal detail belongs in support of decisions; the primary brief should stay impact-first and operational.

## What changed and why this matters now
Cross-border teams increasingly run multiple separations under tight deadlines. In that environment, “we think it is ready” is not a reliable control standard. Teams need a fast way to determine whether a case can safely proceed, what specifically is weak, and who must fix it. A **casefile scorecard** solves this by converting legal and operational requirements into weighted readiness points.

This run uses a scorecard format (distinct from prior watchlist, myth/fact, matrix, switchboard, decision-tree, and checklist-ledger structures) to make go/no-go decisions measurable.

## Plain-language legal context first
China employer-side termination is generally not at-will. It typically requires a lawful route plus coherent process evidence and compliant execution. Core public references include the PRC Labor Contract Law framework, implementing regulation references (including Decree No. 535 context), and Supreme People’s Court labor-dispute interpretation materials. For foreign employers, the practical takeaway is that legality and execution integrity must align: weak route logic, contradictory documentation, or poor payroll closure can each undermine defensibility.

A scorecard model reflects this reality by requiring minimum thresholds before progression.

## Pulse scorecard model (impact-first)

## Readiness scoring dimensions (100 points)
- **Route defensibility (30 points):** lawful basis clarity, fact-route fit, fallback strategy.
- **Evidence integrity (25 points):** chronology quality, source ownership, contradiction resolution.
- **Communication control (15 points):** approved scripts, spokesperson control, record discipline.
- **Payroll/tax closure readiness (20 points):** final pay/severance, IIT/social handling, payment proof plan.
- **Local validation and archive quality (10 points):** city memo and closure retrieval readiness.

## Decision thresholds
- **85-100:** proceed with controlled execution.
- **70-84:** conditional hold; remediation sprint required.
- **<70:** no-go; reframe strategy and route assumptions.

## Pulse rule
No case proceeds if either Route defensibility <20/30 or Payroll/tax closure <14/20, even when total score is above threshold.

## Why this works for foreign employers
Global stakeholders often need confidence signals that are simple, defensible, and comparable across cases. A scorecard enables portfolio-level oversight while preserving legal nuance in the underlying case file. It also reduces subjective conflict between HQ urgency and local compliance warnings by anchoring decisions to transparent thresholds.

## Practical scenarios

### Scenario 1: High total score, hidden payroll weakness
A case scores 86 overall, but payroll/tax readiness is 12/20 due to unresolved settlement mapping. Proceeding creates avoidable correction risk.

**Scorecard outcome:** automatic hold despite high total; payroll lane must be fixed first.

### Scenario 2: Good evidence package, weak route fit
Chronology is strong, but the chosen route rationale is not robust enough for expected challenge.

**Scorecard outcome:** no-go on route threshold; revisit route or alternative strategy.

### Scenario 3: Urgent restructuring push with local variance risk
HQ pushes rapid execution; city-level validation memo is missing.

**Scorecard outcome:** cap score until local validation is complete; escalation required.

## Role-owned SOP (scorecard execution)

## Step 1 — Build baseline score
**Owners:** HRBP + Legal + local reviewer
- Draft route memo and preliminary score by dimension.
- Record assumptions and open risks.
- Assign remediation owners for low dimensions.

## Step 2 — Evidence QA and rescoring
**Owners:** HR Operations + Manager + Compliance
- Complete chronology ledger and conflict checks.
- Update evidence and communication dimension scores.
- Log supporting document IDs for each score change.

## Step 3 — Payroll/tax readiness scoring
**Owners:** Payroll + Tax + HR Ops
- Finalize payout structure and timing.
- Validate IIT/social treatment and payment-proof flow.
- Apply payroll readiness score and lock if below threshold.

## Step 4 — Go/no-go committee check
**Owners:** HR lead + Legal + Payroll lead
- Review total and dimension floor thresholds.
- Decide proceed/hold/no-go with rationale.
- Publish decision note in case file.

## Step 5 — Execution and closure scoring
**Owners:** Compliance + Records + Payroll archive owner
- Capture execution metadata and post-event controls.
- Confirm archive retrieval readiness.
- Finalize closure score and lessons learned tag.

## Required document checklist
- Employee contract/tenure/entity snapshot
- Route rationale memo (+ fallback logic)
- City-level validation memo
- Chronology evidence ledger
- Relevant performance/discipline records
- Script package and approvals
- Payroll/severance calculation worksheet
- IIT/social handling note
- Settlement and acknowledgment records
- Payment proof plan and execution evidence
- Closure memo and indexed archive map

## Exception handling playbook

### Exception A: Score inflation risk (optimistic self-rating)
- **Trigger:** scores not supported by evidence links.
- **Action:** independent reviewer recalibration before decision.

### Exception B: Late contradiction discovery
- **Trigger:** new evidence conflicts with approved narrative.
- **Action:** reopen evidence score, downgrade case, pause communication.

### Exception C: Payroll mismatch post-approval
- **Trigger:** updated calculation changes employee-facing outcome.
- **Action:** immediate rescore, incident note, corrected communication workflow.

### Exception D: Local interpretation conflict
- **Trigger:** inconsistent guidance on route defensibility.
- **Action:** maintain hold status, issue interpretation memo, obtain localized advice.

### Exception E: Manager script deviation during meeting
- **Trigger:** communication departs from approved text.
- **Action:** communication-control score downgrade and same-day legal follow-up.

## System implementation notes
- Add scorecard fields in workflow system with evidence-link requirement.
- Require dual approvals for score edits above set thresholds.
- Implement automatic holds when critical dimension floors are missed.
- Create dashboard: average score by case type, hold reasons, correction rate, dispute outcomes.
- Use quarterly calibration to align scorers across teams and cities.

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** relying on total score only.  
  **Prevention:** enforce critical dimension floors.
- **Mistake:** scoring without evidence linkage.  
  **Prevention:** mandatory document ID references.
- **Mistake:** downgrading local validation importance.  
  **Prevention:** fixed minimum points tied to city memo completion.
- **Mistake:** approving before payroll readiness finalization.  
  **Prevention:** hard lock on payroll floor threshold.
- **Mistake:** no post-case learning loop.  
  **Prevention:** closure score + lessons learned tags.

## What to do now
1. Introduce a 100-point casefile scorecard for all China termination cases.
2. Set mandatory floor thresholds for route and payroll readiness.
3. Require evidence links behind each score component.
4. Add city-validation points that cannot be bypassed.
5. Establish a weekly go/no-go review panel.
6. Track exceptions and recalibrate scoring consistency monthly.
7. Use outcome data to refine weights and controls.

## What to watch next
- Local practice variation may continue to affect scoring benchmarks by city.
- High-volume cycles can increase score inflation unless independent QA is active.
- Practitioner guidance updates may shift what “sufficient evidence” looks like in contested matters.

## FAQ

### 1) Why score cases instead of using a standard checklist only?
Scoring quantifies readiness and allows portfolio comparison while still requiring checklist evidence.

### 2) Can a high score override a weak legal route?
No. Critical floor rules should block progression when route defensibility is below threshold.

### 3) How often should scores be updated?
At every major case transition: route confirmation, evidence completion, payroll readiness, and pre-execution review.

### 4) Who should own final score approval?
A cross-functional group (HR, legal, payroll) rather than a single function.

### 5) Is this model suitable for negotiated separations too?
Yes, with adjusted weights, especially on settlement documentation and payment execution clarity.

### 6) What is the biggest practical benefit?
It reduces subjective arguments and makes hold/proceed decisions auditable.

### 7) What uncertainty remains even with a scorecard?
City- and fact-specific interpretation differences still require localized professional validation.
