# China Employee Termination: Operator Playbook Brief for Foreign Employers (Hourly Test Run)

## Key takeaways
- In China termination cases, legal route selection is only step one; execution control determines whether that route holds under dispute pressure.
- The fastest risk reduction move is to align HR, legal, payroll, and manager messaging before any employee-facing action.
- For foreign employers, a plain-language legal baseline plus a concise operator playbook is more actionable than policy-heavy memos.
- Most preventable failures cluster around evidence inconsistency, settlement-payment mismatch, and uncontrolled manager communication.
- Use a Pulse-style decision brief for leadership, then keep legal depth as appendix support.

## Pulse opening: what decision-makers should focus on now
If your China team asks, “Can we terminate?” the better question is, “Can we execute this route consistently across evidence, communication, and payment?” Foreign employers often lose control not because they chose an impossible route, but because operational handoffs broke the route after approval.

This run rotates to an **operator playbook** structure. It is designed for non-China leadership who need fast clarity: legal context first, practical implications second, role-level execution third. The goal is to compress decision latency while reducing avoidable dispute exposure.

## Plain-language legal context first
China’s Labor Contract Law sets the boundaries for termination pathways and severance logic. The State Council implementation regulations add operational interpretation, while the Supreme People’s Court interpretation frames how disputes are examined in practice. For foreign businesses, the core point is simple: legal route validity and procedural coherence must travel together.

A route that appears available on paper can still become fragile if case records, communication behavior, and settlement execution diverge. In real disputes, inconsistency often matters as much as legal theory.

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- **Board-level:** Require route-plus-execution readiness, not route-only approval.
- **Regional HR:** Treat manager communication as a controlled legal surface, not informal conversation.
- **Payroll/Finance:** Join case planning before meetings; payout and tax mechanics are part of risk control.
- **China leadership:** Build city-calibrated controls while preserving a central governance baseline.

This shift helps multinationals avoid late-stage reversals, because unresolved execution gaps are surfaced before employee engagement rather than after conflict begins.

## Operator playbook: what a “stable” termination process looks like

### 1) Route decision layer (legal fit)
Start with route identification and route conditions. Define what facts must be true for the route to remain viable. If those facts are unresolved, the route stays provisional.

### 2) Execution-readiness layer (operations fit)
Run a compact readiness check before any meeting:
- Evidence chain complete and time-ordered
- Scripted communication package approved
- Settlement components and payment logic pre-verified
- Escalation owner designated

A case that passes legal review but fails readiness should not proceed.

### 3) Closure layer (post-signature integrity)
Do not define closure as “document signed.” Define closure as “payments executed, records archived, and case file complete.” This prevents post-signature disputes driven by process drift.

## Scenario snapshots

### Scenario 1: Legal route available, evidence chain weak
A foreign-invested company identifies a unilateral route that appears legally arguable. During readiness review, the team finds chronology gaps and inconsistent manager records. They pause execution, remediate evidence, and assess negotiated fallback. Outcome: slower start, lower escalation risk.

**Operational lesson:** Route availability does not override evidence quality gates.

### Scenario 2: Negotiated exit, payout explanation unclear
HR secures agreement language, but payroll identifies unresolved treatment assumptions in payout explanation. The team freezes outward communications, aligns calculation notes, and finalizes one employee-facing explanation packet before processing.

**Operational lesson:** Settlement credibility depends on financial and communication coherence.

### Scenario 3: Cross-city complexity under HQ pressure
HQ requests uniform timeline execution across multiple cities. Local counsel flags practice differences in dispute sensitivity. Team keeps one central control pack but adds city-specific escalation notes and approval routing.

**Operational lesson:** Standardization should be control-led, not context-blind.

## Daily operating SOP (role ownership)
1. **HR Case Owner:** Intake facts, classify route options, and open risk log.
2. **Legal/Counsel:** Confirm legal boundaries and mandatory preconditions.
3. **Payroll Lead:** Build settlement model, payment schedule, and tax treatment assumptions.
4. **Manager:** Receive approved communication lines and prohibited statements.
5. **HR Ops:** Assemble evidence index, confirm timestamps, and verify document integrity.
6. **Control Gate Reviewer:** Approve go/no-go based on legal + readiness threshold.
7. **Execution Team:** Conduct communication and settlement steps with documented outputs.
8. **Payroll/Finance:** Process payment, capture proof package, and reconcile ledger.
9. **Archive Owner:** Close only after completeness validation across legal, HR, and payroll files.

## Document checklist foreign employers should enforce
- Case intake and route memo
- Evidence chronology index and owner sign-off
- Communication script pack (including approved translation where needed)
- Settlement worksheet (components, dates, assumptions)
- Legal and payroll pre-action approval log
- Employee meeting outcome records
- Payment proof set and reconciliation log
- Final archive map with exception notes

## Exception playbook (high-frequency issues)
- **Evidence gap appears late:** pause execution; assign remediation owner and deadline.
- **Manager narrative drifts:** issue corrected written summary; record deviation and counsel review.
- **Employee disputes payout detail:** provide itemized breakdown and legal/payroll joint explanation.
- **Protected-status concern emerges:** halt process and escalate immediately.
- **Cross-functional disagreement on route:** escalate to designated decision authority with written risk summary.

## System implementation notes (controls and auditability)
In workflow tooling, enforce required fields:
- Route basis and legal condition status
- Evidence completeness and unresolved gap counter
- Script version and delivery timestamp
- Settlement version lock and payroll reviewer signature
- Escalation owner and decision timestamp
- Closure proof checklist and archive hash

Recommended controls:
- Hard stop when evidence or settlement lock is incomplete
- Mandatory re-approval if route or payout assumptions change
- Immutable audit trail for approvals and document versions
- Weekly dashboard for unresolved exceptions and closure lag

## Common mistakes and prevention moves
- **Mistake:** Route approved without readiness gate.  
  **Prevention:** Require legal fit + execution fit sign-off.
- **Mistake:** Payroll consulted after communication begins.  
  **Prevention:** Make payroll pre-brief mandatory.
- **Mistake:** Manager messaging improvised.  
  **Prevention:** One script owner, one briefing, one final version.
- **Mistake:** Closure declared at signature.  
  **Prevention:** Closure requires payment proof + archive completion.
- **Mistake:** One-size-fits-all city handling.  
  **Prevention:** Central framework with local escalation overlays.

## FAQ
### Is this playbook a legal requirement?
No. It is an operational governance model to improve defensibility and execution quality.

### Can smaller foreign teams use this approach?
Yes. Start with a simplified gate: route memo, evidence index, settlement lock, and closure proof.

### Why is payroll included so early?
Because payout design and tax handling affect both trust and dispute exposure.

### Is negotiated separation always safer?
Often lower-friction, but only when documentation and payment execution are controlled.

### Do we need city-level differences documented each time?
Use a standard baseline, then add city-specific notes where practice variation is material.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring situations, and multi-city complexity.
