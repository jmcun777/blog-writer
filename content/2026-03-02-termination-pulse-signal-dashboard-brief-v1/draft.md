# China Employee Termination: Signal Dashboard Brief for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers should treat China termination risk as a **signal management problem**, not only a legal-route choice.
- Three leading indicators drive most adverse outcomes: evidence inconsistency, communication drift, and settlement-payment mismatch.
- A compact Pulse-style dashboard helps HQ make faster decisions without losing legal discipline.
- Legal rules are the baseline, but execution coherence is the strongest predictor of dispute resilience.
- Keep legal detail in an appendix; lead business teams with clear indicators and actions.

## Pulse headline view
For overseas operators, the practical challenge in China terminations is rarely “no legal route exists.” The challenge is that a route can degrade during execution when internal signals are ignored. If warning signals are tracked early, teams can reroute or remediate before disputes harden.

This run uses an indicator-led format: a **termination signal dashboard**. It gives non-specialist leaders a plain-language legal baseline first, then a concise decision layer focused on what to monitor this week.

## Plain-language legal context first
China’s Labor Contract Law provides route boundaries for termination and severance obligations. The implementation regulations and Supreme People’s Court interpretation influence how disputes are assessed in practice, particularly around process fairness, documentary coherence, and proof reliability. For foreign employers, the direct implication is clear: legal route validity and operating discipline must remain synchronized from decision to payment completion.

In practice, dispute exposure often grows where process signals were visible but untreated. A legal route can be theoretically available while operationally unstable.

## Termination signal dashboard (foreign-employer edition)

### Signal 1: Evidence Integrity (Leading)
**What it means:** Are records complete, consistent, and chronology-aligned with the stated route?

**Why it matters:** Weak evidence quality is a frequent route destabilizer in disputes.

**Trigger thresholds:**
- Green: complete chronology and owner-validated index
- Amber: minor gaps, remediable before action
- Red: core contradictions or missing critical records

**Immediate action:** No employee-facing step should proceed while signal is red.

### Signal 2: Communication Control (Leading)
**What it means:** Are manager and HR narratives aligned to approved language and route logic?

**Why it matters:** Informal or inconsistent statements can undercut otherwise valid process steps.

**Trigger thresholds:**
- Green: one approved script + briefing complete
- Amber: script exists but training incomplete
- Red: ad-lib communication risk is high

**Immediate action:** Hold meeting and run mandatory manager alignment briefing.

### Signal 3: Settlement-Payroll Coherence (Leading)
**What it means:** Are settlement components, payment timing, and tax assumptions internally consistent?

**Why it matters:** Payment ambiguity often becomes post-signature friction.

**Trigger thresholds:**
- Green: settlement version locked, payroll confirmed
- Amber: minor calc clarifications pending
- Red: unresolved component or tax logic conflict

**Immediate action:** Freeze external commitments until payroll/legal reconciliation is complete.

### Signal 4: Escalation Readiness (Lag-prevention)
**What it means:** Is there a named owner and path for high-risk exceptions?

**Why it matters:** Delayed escalation converts manageable issues into formal disputes.

**Trigger thresholds:**
- Green: owner + timeline + authority defined
- Amber: owner named, authority unclear
- Red: no practical escalation path

**Immediate action:** Assign authority and approval rights before execution.

## Practical implications by stakeholder
- **HQ/Board:** Ask for signal status, not just legal memo conclusions.
- **Regional HR:** Use dashboard status to decide pause/proceed, not calendar pressure.
- **China HR Ops:** Maintain evidence and script discipline as core controls.
- **Payroll/Finance:** Validate settlement mechanics before communication commitments.
- **Managers:** Follow approved language; deviations are a measurable risk event.

## Scenario snapshots

### Snapshot A: “Legal green, operational red” case
A company had legal confidence in route selection, but evidence signal moved to red after chronology review. Team paused action, remediated records, and updated risk note to HQ. Result: delayed launch, lower contest risk.

### Snapshot B: “Signed but unstable” case
Agreement was signed, but settlement-payment signal turned red due to unresolved treatment logic. Team paused payment communication, reconciled terms, and documented employee explanation. Result: closure integrity recovered.

### Snapshot C: “Multi-city noise” case
One process applied to several cities without local calibration. Escalation signal turned amber/red in higher-variance cities. Team adopted central minimum controls plus local escalation overlays.

## Daily operations SOP (signal-led)
1. Open case with provisional route and mandatory signal baseline.
2. Validate evidence signal with owner-signed chronology index.
3. Approve communication package and manager briefing completion.
4. Lock settlement worksheet and payroll/tax assumption notes.
5. Run go/no-go gate using four-signal status.
6. Execute with real-time exception logging.
7. Complete payment proof and closure audit before final case close.

## Document checklist
- Route memo and condition log
- Evidence index + chronology map
- Communication script and manager briefing record
- Settlement worksheet + payroll check
- Approval log and escalation matrix
- Meeting outcome records
- Payment proof and reconciliation pack
- Final archive completeness report

## Exception handling playbook
- **Evidence red:** stop route execution; remediate with owner and deadline.
- **Communication drift event:** issue corrected written line, log deviation, review risk impact.
- **Settlement mismatch:** pause external commitment, reconcile and relock settlement version.
- **Escalation gap:** appoint authority immediately; no further action until confirmed.
- **Local-variance conflict:** add city-specific counsel note while preserving baseline control set.

## System implementation notes
Recommended workflow fields:
- route basis and status
- signal scores (evidence/communication/settlement/escalation)
- unresolved issue counter and owner
- settlement lock version and payroll reviewer
- exception log with timestamps
- closure completeness score

Control mechanics:
- Hard-stop automation on any red leading signal.
- Re-approval required when route basis or settlement assumptions change.
- Immutable audit trail for approvals, scripts, and settlement versions.
- Weekly dashboard to leadership with red-signal aging and repeat root causes.

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** Relying on legal route alone.  
  **Prevention:** Require signal dashboard clearance before proceed.
- **Mistake:** Treating manager communication as soft skill issue.  
  **Prevention:** Classify communication drift as a control breach.
- **Mistake:** Late payroll involvement.  
  **Prevention:** Mandatory pre-action payroll review gate.
- **Mistake:** Closing case at signature.  
  **Prevention:** Closure only after payment proof and archive completion.

## FAQ
### Is this dashboard legally required?
No. It is an operations governance model to improve execution quality and defensibility.

### Can this work for small China teams?
Yes. Start with evidence, communication, and settlement signals only, then add escalation signal.

### Why lead with indicators?
Because indicators convert legal and operational risk into actionable decisions for non-specialists.

### Is negotiated separation always lower risk?
Often lower-friction, but only when settlement and communication signals remain green.

### When should external counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring contexts, and multi-city complexity.
