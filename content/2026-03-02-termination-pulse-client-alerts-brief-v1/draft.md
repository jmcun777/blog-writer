# China Employee Termination: Client Alerts Brief for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers should run China termination decisions through an alert system, not static memos.
- The most damaging failures are usually known in advance: route drift, evidence contradictions, script drift, payout mismatch, and premature closure.
- Pulse-style alert briefs improve readability and speed for non-China leadership.
- Legal route and execution readiness are separate gates; both must be green.
- Legal depth belongs in an appendix while practical alerts lead.

## Pulse lead
This run rotates to a **client alerts** format. The objective is simple: if a risk appears, leadership should know the trigger, impact, and exact next action in one glance.

## Plain-language legal/regulatory context first
China’s Labor Contract Law defines route boundaries and severance obligations. Implementation regulations and SPC interpretation shape how disputes test fairness, documentary reliability, and consistency between employer rationale and execution records. Practical implication for foreign operators: route legality must stay aligned with evidence, communication, and settlement execution through final closure.

## Alert board (trigger → impact → action)

### Alert 1: Route drift detected
- **Trigger:** new facts conflict with route assumptions
- **Impact:** legal fit degrades quickly
- **Action:** rerun route approval; hold next step until revalidated

### Alert 2: Critical chronology contradiction
- **Trigger:** missing/contradictory core record
- **Impact:** dispute defensibility weakens
- **Action:** pause employee-facing activity; assign remediation owner + SLA

### Alert 3: Script-control break
- **Trigger:** manager message diverges from approved script
- **Impact:** narrative inconsistency risk
- **Action:** corrected written summary + legal impact review

### Alert 4: Settlement mapping mismatch
- **Trigger:** worksheet and legal terms misaligned
- **Impact:** post-signature dispute/reopen risk
- **Action:** relock worksheet with legal/payroll co-sign before commitment

### Alert 5: Tax-treatment inconsistency
- **Trigger:** conflicting payout/tax explanations
- **Impact:** trust and compliance risk
- **Action:** unify tax note; validate assumptions locally as needed

### Alert 6: Escalation authority gap
- **Trigger:** no empowered approver at cutover
- **Impact:** rushed or stalled execution
- **Action:** activate backup authority before proceeding

### Alert 7: Premature closure signal
- **Trigger:** signature logged without payment proof/archive completion
- **Impact:** unresolved downstream exposure
- **Action:** reopen closure status; complete proof-based gate

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- **HQ:** request alert status and owner SLAs, not memo-only updates.
- **Regional HR:** treat unresolved critical alerts as automatic no-go.
- **Payroll/finance:** co-own Alerts 4/5 before employee commitments.
- **Managers:** script discipline is a compliance control.

## Daily SOP (alert-driven)
1. Initialize alerts 1-7 for each active case.
2. Assign owner/SLA to each active alert.
3. Run legal/payroll parallel checks for alerts 4/5.
4. Hold progression on unresolved critical alerts.
5. Execute with live alert log updates.
6. Close only when Alert 7 criteria are complete.

## FAQ
### Is an alert board legally required?
No. It is an operational governance model.

### Can smaller teams use this?
Yes. Start with alerts 1-5.

### Why is this useful for foreign clients?
It converts legal complexity into fast action rules.

### Is negotiated termination always safer?
Not automatically; execution coherence still drives risk.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring, and multi-city complexity.

## Governance escalation ladder (who decides when)
To keep alerts actionable, map each critical alert to decision authority:
- Tier 1: HR case owner (initial hold/release recommendation)
- Tier 2: Legal + payroll joint owner (technical risk validation)
- Tier 3: Regional decision authority (final go/no-go at cutover)
- Tier 4: Executive escalation (material business impact or repeated control failures)

No case should move from Tier 2 to execution without explicit Tier 3 confirmation.

## City-level calibration for foreign operators
Use a country baseline plus city overlays:
- local counsel trigger threshold
- local evidence sensitivity reminders
- local settlement communication cautions
- local dispute timeline expectation

This approach preserves consistency while reducing over-standardized mistakes.

## Leadership one-page output template
Every bridge/alert review should end with a one-page decision record:
- active alerts and severity
- owners + SLA deadlines
- go/no-go decision with rationale
- unresolved residual risk statement
- next checkpoint time and responsible owner

This keeps cross-border decisions comparable and audit-ready.

## Quality guardrails before execution
- no unresolved critical alerts
- no missing owner or SLA
- no mismatch between settlement worksheet and communication copy
- no closure status before payment proof and archive completion


## Weekly operating rhythm (practical checklist)
1. Review all open critical alerts and aging status.
2. Confirm owner accountability and SLA recovery plans.
3. Identify repeated alert combinations (e.g., Alert 2 + Alert 4).
4. Decide control adjustment actions for next cycle.

## Monthly control recalibration workshop
Run a 60-minute cross-functional workshop with HR, legal, payroll, and regional leadership:
- review top three recurring alert clusters
- identify one structural root cause per cluster
- implement one preventive control change per cluster
- assign owner and due date for each change
- validate whether prior month controls reduced recurrence

## KPI intervention matrix
- **Green:** KPI stable or improving → continue current control settings.
- **Amber:** KPI deteriorates one cycle → increase monitoring frequency.
- **Red:** KPI deteriorates two cycles → mandatory corrective plan and leadership sign-off.

This matrix prevents delayed intervention when risk trends are visible.
