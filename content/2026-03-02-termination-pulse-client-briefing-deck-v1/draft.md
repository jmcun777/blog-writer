# China Employee Termination: Client Briefing Deck for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers should treat China terminations as a controlled execution program, not a one-step legal decision.
- The most expensive failures are usually cross-functional: legal route, evidence chain, manager communication, and payout mechanics fall out of sync.
- A Pulse-style client briefing deck improves decision speed because each section answers one board-level question in plain language.
- Legal sufficiency and operational readiness are separate tests; both must pass.
- Keep legal depth in an appendix and lead with decision-useful controls.

## Pulse headline (for fast readers)
If your team is making a China termination decision today, the right question is not only “can we terminate?” It is “can we execute this route coherently from first decision to final payment proof?”

This run uses a **client briefing deck format** for foreign operators: headline first, then concise slides-as-sections with clear implications.

## Slide 1 — What changed for foreign employers
The risk profile has not simplified. National legal anchors are stable, but dispute outcomes continue to reward procedural coherence and punish control drift. For foreign clients, this means route choice alone is no longer a sufficient proxy for risk quality.

**Implication:** move from route-only approvals to route-plus-execution approvals.

## Slide 2 — Plain-language legal/regulatory baseline
China’s Labor Contract Law sets route boundaries and severance principles. The implementation regulations and SPC interpretation shape how disputes assess evidence reliability, fairness, and consistency across actions. In practical terms: if your route narrative, documentation, communication, and payout execution diverge, defensibility declines.

**Implication:** legal and operations teams must co-own case quality from intake through closure.

## Slide 3 — The four control questions leadership should ask
1. **Route integrity:** are route prerequisites still true as facts evolve?
2. **Evidence coherence:** does a clean chronology support the route narrative?
3. **Settlement clarity:** are payout components and timing unambiguous?
4. **Execution closure:** do payment proof and archive checks match closure claims?

**Implication:** unresolved “no” answers should trigger hold, not workaround.

## Slide 4 — Foreign-client risk map (impact-first)
- **High impact / high frequency:** evidence contradictions, script drift, payout ambiguity
- **High impact / lower frequency:** protected-status findings near cutover, missing authority at go-live
- **Medium impact / high frequency:** delayed payroll alignment, inconsistent closure records

**Implication:** prioritize controls where impact and recurrence overlap.

## Slide 5 — Practical governance model (who owns what)
- **HQ or regional leadership:** final route-plus-execution approval standards
- **HR case owner:** chronology integrity and escalation activation
- **Legal owner:** route validity, evidence sufficiency, procedural fit
- **Payroll owner:** payout/tax coherence and payment execution integrity
- **Manager owner:** script control and meeting-record discipline
- **Control owner:** closure gate and archive completeness

**Implication:** ambiguity in ownership is itself a risk event.

## Slide 6 — Scenario snapshots foreign teams recognize
### Scenario A: Route green, evidence amber
Legal fit appears acceptable; chronology conflict appears late. Team pauses, remediates, and reruns approval. Outcome: short delay, stronger defensibility.

### Scenario B: Settlement red before communication
Payroll identifies component mismatch before employee meeting. Team relocks worksheet and explanation note. Outcome: lower post-signature friction.

### Scenario C: Escalation owner unavailable
Decision authority absent during cutover. Backup chain activates and holds progression. Outcome: avoids uncontrolled execution.

## Slide 7 — Daily operating SOP (deck version)
1. Open case with route assumptions and control checklist.
2. Build owner-verified chronology and contradiction log.
3. Lock communication script and manager briefing proof.
4. Co-sign settlement/tax worksheet before commitments.
5. Run go/no-go gate on unresolved critical risks.
6. Execute with exception logging and change control.
7. Close only with payment proof + archive verification.

## Slide 8 — Required checklist foreign teams can audit
- route memo + prerequisites
- chronology index + unresolved contradictions
- script pack + manager acknowledgment
- settlement worksheet + tax note
- escalation matrix (primary + backup)
- payment proof + reconciliation bundle
- closure audit and archive record

## Slide 9 — KPI panel for weekly governance
- unresolved critical-risk count
- average risk aging over SLA
- script-drift incident frequency
- settlement relock rate
- closure lag (signature to verified close)
- post-close reopen rate

If two or more KPIs worsen for two cycles, trigger control recalibration.

## Slide 10 — What foreign clients should do this week
1. Replace route-only approvals with route-plus-execution gating.
2. Enforce payroll/legal co-sign before employee commitments.
3. Add automatic holds for unresolved critical contradictions.
4. Require backup decision authority for cutover windows.
5. Run weekly KPI review and monthly pattern recalibration.

## FAQ
### Is this deck format legally required?
No. It is an operating format to improve decision quality and execution discipline.

### Can smaller foreign teams use it?
Yes. Start with Slides 2-4 and Slide 7 controls.

### Why lead with operational controls?
Because legal validity without process coherence still creates dispute risk.

### Is mutual termination always low risk?
Not automatically; documentation and payout execution still decide stability.

### When is external PRC counsel mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring, and multi-city complexity.
