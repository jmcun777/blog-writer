# China Employee Termination: Escalation Bulletin Brief for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers should manage China termination risk with a standing escalation bulletin, not static memo workflows.
- Most preventable disputes come from recurring escalation triggers: route drift, evidence contradiction, script drift, settlement mismatch, and premature closure.
- Pulse-style bulletin format improves readability and decision speed for non-China leadership.
- Legal route approval and execution readiness remain separate gates and must both pass.
- Legal depth should remain secondary in an appendix while operational actions lead.

## Pulse lead
This run rotates to an **escalation bulletin** format: each bulletin item tells leadership what changed, why it matters, and what action is mandatory now.

## Plain-language legal/regulatory context first
China’s Labor Contract Law defines termination boundaries and severance obligations. State Council implementation regulations and SPC interpretation shape how disputes test procedural fairness, documentary reliability, and narrative consistency between stated rationale and execution records. For foreign operators, practical meaning is straightforward: legal route and execution controls must remain aligned through closure.

## Escalation bulletin board (change → impact → mandatory action)

### Bulletin 1: Route assumption change
- **Change:** new facts conflict with original route assumptions
- **Impact:** legal-fit confidence declines rapidly
- **Mandatory action:** rerun route gate and pause progression until reapproved

### Bulletin 2: Evidence contradiction opened
- **Change:** core chronology mismatch or missing record discovered
- **Impact:** defensibility risk increases significantly
- **Mandatory action:** hold employee-facing steps; assign remediation owner and SLA

### Bulletin 3: Communication variance detected
- **Change:** manager narrative diverges from approved script
- **Impact:** inconsistent record undermines case coherence
- **Mandatory action:** issue corrected written summary and legal impact review

### Bulletin 4: Settlement component mismatch
- **Change:** payroll mapping and legal wording no longer aligned
- **Impact:** post-signature dispute/reopen risk rises
- **Mandatory action:** relock worksheet with legal/payroll co-sign before commitment

### Bulletin 5: Tax explanation conflict
- **Change:** differing treatment explanations across teams
- **Impact:** trust, compliance, and payout clarity risk
- **Mandatory action:** unify tax note and validate local assumptions if needed

### Bulletin 6: Authority coverage gap
- **Change:** no active decision owner at cutover
- **Impact:** rushed or stalled execution risk
- **Mandatory action:** activate backup authority before any next step

### Bulletin 7: Closure evidence gap
- **Change:** case marked closed without payment proof/archive completeness
- **Impact:** unresolved legal/operational exposure remains
- **Mandatory action:** reopen status and complete proof-based closure gate

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- **HQ/board:** ask for current bulletin items and owner SLAs, not route-only summaries.
- **Regional HR:** unresolved critical bulletin items should trigger automatic no-go.
- **Payroll/finance:** co-own settlement/tax bulletin items before employee commitments.
- **Managers:** script discipline is compliance behavior, not optional style.

## Daily SOP (bulletin-driven)
1. Initialize bulletin items 1-7 per active case.
2. Assign owner/SLA and severity to each active item.
3. Run legal/payroll parallel checks for items 4 and 5.
4. Hold progression on unresolved critical items.
5. Execute with live bulletin updates.
6. Close only when bulletin 7 criteria pass.

If 2+ KPIs worsen for 2 cycles, trigger corrective cross-functional review.

## FAQ
### Is an escalation bulletin legally required?
No. It is an operational governance model.

### Can smaller teams use it?
Yes. Start with items 1-5.

### Why is this useful for foreign clients?
It converts legal complexity into fast, owner-based action.

### Is negotiated termination always safer?
Not automatically; execution coherence remains decisive.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring, and multi-city complexity.

## City-overlay calibration for multinational teams
Use a two-layer control model:
- **Country baseline:** route, evidence, script, settlement, closure minimum controls
- **City overlay:** local counsel trigger points, documentation sensitivity, dispute timeline assumptions

This helps foreign teams avoid over-standardization while preserving governance consistency.

## Leadership one-page output format
Every escalation cycle should end with a one-page summary:
- active bulletin items + severity
- owner and SLA by item
- go/no-go recommendation with rationale
- residual risk statement
- next checkpoint time and decision owner

This keeps cross-border decisions comparable and audit-ready.


## Weekly operating checklist (execution discipline)
1. Review all open critical bulletin items and aging status.
2. Confirm owner accountability and SLA recovery plans.
3. Identify recurring item combinations (e.g., route drift + evidence contradiction).
4. Decide one control change for next cycle.
5. Log lessons learned and update city overlays.

## Monthly recalibration workshop
Run a 60-minute cross-functional review:
- top recurring escalation clusters
- root-cause diagnosis per cluster
- one preventive control change per cluster
- owner + deadline assignment
- validation of prior control effectiveness

## KPI intervention matrix
- **Green:** stable/improving → maintain settings
- **Amber:** one-cycle deterioration → increase monitoring frequency
- **Red:** two-cycle deterioration → mandatory corrective plan with leadership sign-off

This prevents delayed intervention when risk trends are visible.
