# China Employee Termination: Decision Desk Brief for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers should run a structured decision-desk check before any China termination action.
- The biggest avoidable failures are decision hygiene issues: stale route assumptions, unresolved evidence contradictions, and payout communication mismatches.
- Pulse-style decision-desk formatting improves clarity for non-China leadership.
- Legal route validity and execution readiness are dual gates.
- Legal depth remains in appendix while operational checks lead.

## Pulse lead
This run rotates to a **decision desk** format: a compact set of pre-action checks that leadership can review quickly and enforce consistently.

## Plain-language legal/regulatory context first
China’s Labor Contract Law sets route boundaries and severance obligations. State Council implementation regulations and SPC interpretation shape dispute outcomes by testing procedural fairness, documentary reliability, and consistency between employer rationale and execution records. For foreign operators, this means route, evidence, communication, and settlement logic must stay coherent through closure.

## Decision desk checks (question → red flag → required action)

### Check 1: Route fit still valid?
- **Red flag:** new facts invalidate route assumptions
- **Required action:** rerun legal gate; hold execution until refreshed

### Check 2: Evidence chain coherent?
- **Red flag:** chronology contradiction unresolved
- **Required action:** assign owner/SLA and block employee-facing step

### Check 3: Communication lock confirmed?
- **Red flag:** script not finalized or manager variance observed
- **Required action:** lock script and briefing confirmation before meeting

### Check 4: Settlement mapping aligned?
- **Red flag:** payroll worksheet and legal terms mismatch
- **Required action:** legal/payroll co-sign relock before commitments

### Check 5: Tax note unified?
- **Red flag:** inconsistent payout/tax wording across teams
- **Required action:** unify note and validate local assumptions as needed

### Check 6: Authority coverage active?
- **Red flag:** missing primary/backup approver at cutover
- **Required action:** confirm decision chain before go/no-go

### Check 7: Closure proof complete?
- **Red flag:** signature logged without payment proof/archive checks
- **Required action:** keep case open until proof gate passes

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- **HQ:** request decision-desk output rather than route-only summaries.
- **Regional HR:** unresolved critical checks should auto-trigger no-go.
- **Payroll/finance:** co-own checks 4 and 5 before commitments.
- **Managers:** script discipline remains compliance-critical.

## Scenario snapshots
- Route refreshed after fact change avoided stale execution.
- Evidence contradiction cleared pre-meeting prevented avoidable escalation.
- Settlement relock prevented post-signature payout dispute.

## Daily SOP (decision desk)
1. Run checks 1-7 at cycle start.
2. Assign owner/SLA for open critical items.
3. Hold execution until critical items clear.
4. Record go/hold/escalate decision with rationale.
5. Re-run desk checks after material fact changes.
6. Close only with proof-based closure gate.

## FAQ
### Is decision-desk governance legally required?
No. It is an operational governance model.

### Can small teams use this?
Yes. Start with checks 1-5.

### Why useful for foreign clients?
It converts legal complexity into clear operating checks.

### Is negotiated termination always safer?
Not automatically; execution coherence remains decisive.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring, and multi-city complexity.
