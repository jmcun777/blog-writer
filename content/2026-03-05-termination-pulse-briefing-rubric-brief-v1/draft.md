# China Employee Termination 2026: Pulse Briefing-Rubric Brief for Foreign Employers

## Key takeaways
- A compact **briefing rubric** helps foreign teams judge China termination readiness consistently across cases.
- The highest-impact rubric dimensions are route legality, evidence coherence, payroll/tax closure, communication discipline, and local interpretation fit.
- Rubric-based reviews reduce subjective “looks fine” decisions.
- City-level interpretation must be scored explicitly, not assumed.
- This brief is impact-first; legal depth follows in a secondary appendix.

## What changed and why this matters now
Distributed teams often make different decisions on similar facts because they lack a shared quality bar. One team calls a case ready at 70% completion; another holds until 95%. A briefing rubric resolves this by defining common scoring dimensions, minimum thresholds, and automatic hold triggers.

This run rotates to a **briefing-rubric format** (distinct from prior sprint, card, handbook, and ladder variants) to improve consistency and Pulse-style readability.

## Plain-language legal context first
China employer-side termination generally requires a lawful basis, coherent factual support, and compliant execution sequencing. Public legal anchors include the PRC Labor Contract Law framework, implementing regulation references (including Decree No. 535 context), and Supreme People’s Court labor-dispute interpretation materials. Practical takeaway: legal entitlement and operational quality are inseparable.

A rubric framework translates this into comparable, evidence-based decisions.

## Pulse briefing rubric (impact-first)

## Dimension 1 — Route Legality (30)
**Measures:** fact-route fit, route memo specificity, fallback design.

**Hold trigger:** score below 22/30.

## Dimension 2 — Local Interpretation Fit (15)
**Measures:** city memo quality, unresolved local issue status.

**Hold trigger:** unresolved conflicting local guidance.

## Dimension 3 — Evidence Coherence (20)
**Measures:** chronology integrity, source ownership, contradiction closure.

**Hold trigger:** material contradiction open.

## Dimension 4 — Payroll/Tax Closure (20)
**Measures:** pay/severance finalization, IIT/social mapping, payment-proof readiness.

**Hold trigger:** unresolved payout/tax element.

## Dimension 5 — Communication Discipline (10)
**Measures:** script approval, spokesperson briefing, deviation protocol.

**Hold trigger:** unbriefed speaker or high drift risk.

## Dimension 6 — Closure Resilience (5)
**Measures:** indexed archive and retrieval test.

**Hold trigger:** retrieval test not passed.

## Rubric decision bands
- **85–100:** proceed with controlled execution.
- **70–84:** conditional hold, remediation required.
- **<70:** no-go and route/process redesign.

**Critical rule:** any hold trigger overrides total score.

## Why this helps foreign employers
A rubric creates one language for HQ, local HR, legal, and payroll teams. It speeds governance while reducing cross-functional disputes over readiness. It also supports portfolio comparison across multiple active cases.

## Practical scenarios

### Scenario 1: High total score, local fit unresolved
Case scores 87 overall but local guidance conflicts remain open.

**Rubric response:** hold due to Dimension 2 trigger.

### Scenario 2: Strong route and evidence, payroll weak
Legal and documentation are solid; payout mapping still incomplete.

**Rubric response:** hold due to Dimension 4 trigger.

### Scenario 3: Near-complete case, communication control weak
Script exists but spokesperson not fully briefed.

**Rubric response:** hold until Dimension 5 control is complete.

## Role-owned SOP (rubric governance)

## Step 1 — Initialize rubric scoring
**Owners:** HRBP + Legal + Payroll lead
- Assign scorers and evidence owners per dimension.

## Step 2 — Score with evidence links
**Owners:** dimension leads
- Record score plus proof for each dimension.

## Step 3 — Apply trigger checks
**Owners:** cross-functional panel
- Enforce hold rules regardless of total score.

## Step 4 — Publish decision memo
**Owners:** HR + Legal + Payroll
- Document proceed/hold/no-go and remediation tasks.

## Step 5 — Monthly calibration
**Owners:** operations governance + compliance
- Review scoring consistency and adjust criteria.

## Required document checklist
- Employee profile and contract context
- Route rationale memo + fallback route
- City-level interpretation memo
- Chronology/source ledger
- Script package and briefing records
- Payroll/severance/IIT/social worksheet
- Settlement/acknowledgment records
- Payment proof artifacts
- Rubric scoring log
- Closure memo + archive index

## Exception handling playbook
- **Score dispute across functions:** tie-break memo with evidence.
- **New fact after scoring:** reopen affected dimensions and rescore.
- **Owner unavailable:** activate backup scorer and retain SLA.
- **Deadline override request:** document unresolved triggers and risk acceptance.
- **Repeated low score in one dimension:** launch targeted process fix.

## System implementation notes
- Add dimension score fields with evidence-link requirement.
- Auto-block progression when triggers fire.
- Track KPIs: dimension-level failure rate, time-to-remediation, dispute outcomes.
- Add audit trail for score changes.
- Recalibrate thresholds quarterly.

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** total-score fixation.  
  **Prevention:** enforce trigger override rules.
- **Mistake:** scoring without evidence.  
  **Prevention:** mandatory proof-linked scoring.
- **Mistake:** local fit underweighted.  
  **Prevention:** separate Dimension 2 gate.
- **Mistake:** payroll scored too late.  
  **Prevention:** early Dimension 4 scoring checkpoint.
- **Mistake:** no calibration loop.  
  **Prevention:** monthly scorer alignment.

## What to do now
1. Deploy a six-dimension rubric for all active China termination cases.
2. Define trigger thresholds and override rules.
3. Require evidence-linked scoring in every review.
4. Enforce local-fit and payroll triggers as hard gates.
5. Run daily rubric updates during active handling.
6. Track repeat low-scoring dimensions.
7. Use calibration reviews to improve scoring consistency.

## What to watch next

## FAQ

### 1) Why use a rubric instead of ad hoc judgment?
It standardizes decisions and reduces subjective variance.

### 2) Can one trigger block a high total score?
Yes. Trigger overrides are intentional risk controls.

### 3) How often should scoring occur?
At each major transition and after material updates.

### 4) Who approves final status?
Cross-functional HR, legal, and payroll decision-makers.

### 5) Is city-level fit always required?
Yes, because local practice can materially affect outcomes.

### 6) What is the top avoidable miss?
Proceeding while payroll/local/evidence triggers remain open.

### 7) How does the rubric improve over time?
By tracking dimension failures and refining controls.
