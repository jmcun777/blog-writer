# China Employee Termination 2026: Pulse Decision-Cards Brief for Foreign Employers

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers can improve China termination outcomes by using a compact set of **decision cards** at each critical control point.
- The four highest-impact cards are route validity, evidence coherence, payroll/tax closure, and communication control.
- Card-based governance reduces ambiguity in fast cross-border decisions.
- City-level interpretation should appear as a mandatory card, not optional commentary.
- This brief leads with practical actions; legal depth is secondary in the appendix.

## What changed and why this matters now
When teams move quickly, long SOPs are often ignored in real time. Decision cards solve this by shrinking complex control requirements into high-signal prompts with explicit pass/fail criteria. For foreign operators who manage China cases across time zones, card logic improves handoffs: each card shows what must be true before progression.

This run rotates to a **decision-cards format** (distinct from operator handbook, readiness ladder, and prior structures) to keep the narrative concise and execution-ready.

## Plain-language legal context first
China employer-initiated termination generally requires a lawful basis plus coherent evidence and process execution. Public anchors include the PRC Labor Contract Law framework, implementing regulation references (including Decree No. 535 context), and Supreme People’s Court labor-dispute interpretation materials. Practical implication: route legality and operational discipline must stay synchronized.

Decision cards operationalize this by forcing explicit pass/fail checks before each move.

## Pulse decision cards (impact-first)

## Card 1 — Route Validity
**Question:** Does the chosen route fit this fact pattern and have a fallback?

**Pass evidence:** route memo + fallback path + owner sign-off.

**Fail signal:** generic rationale, unresolved route assumptions.

**Action on fail:** stop and rework route.

## Card 2 — Local Interpretation
**Question:** Is city-level practical interpretation resolved?

**Pass evidence:** local memo + open-issue tracker status.

**Fail signal:** conflicting local guidance unresolved.

**Action on fail:** hold progression pending localized resolution.

## Card 3 — Evidence Coherence
**Question:** Can chronology and source records withstand challenge?

**Pass evidence:** contradiction-free ledger with source ownership.

**Fail signal:** timeline gaps, conflicting statements.

**Action on fail:** remediation sprint before any communication prep.

## Card 4 — Payroll/Tax Closure
**Question:** Are final pay, severance, IIT/social treatment, and payment proof fully ready?

**Pass evidence:** approved calc pack + payment workflow.

**Fail signal:** unresolved payout/tax element.

**Action on fail:** no-go until closure complete.

## Card 5 — Communication Control
**Question:** Is messaging aligned and spokesperson discipline confirmed?

**Pass evidence:** approved script + briefing log + fallback spokesperson.

**Fail signal:** unbriefed manager, script drift risk.

**Action on fail:** block meeting scheduling.

## Card 6 — Closure Readiness
**Question:** Can the case be reconstructed quickly post-event?

**Pass evidence:** indexed archive + retrieval test result.

**Fail signal:** fragmented files, missing index links.

**Action on fail:** keep case open until closure quality passes.

## Why this helps foreign employers
Decision cards are easy to use in executive updates and operator huddles. They make control status visible without requiring long legal reviews and reduce disagreement by using concrete pass/fail evidence.

## Practical scenarios

### Scenario 1: Legal route approved, local card fails
National-level logic appears sound, but local interpretation remains unclear.

**Card response:** Card 2 fail blocks progression.

### Scenario 2: Evidence card fails near communication date
A late contradiction appears between manager notes and chronology.

**Card response:** Card 3 fail triggers remediation; communication is paused.

### Scenario 3: Payroll card fails after script approval
Communication plan exists, but final IIT mapping is unresolved.

**Card response:** Card 4 fail overrides scheduling pressure.

## Role-owned SOP (card governance)

## Step 1 — Assign card owners
**Owners:** HRBP + Legal + Payroll lead
- Define owner and backup per card.

## Step 2 — Run card checks in order
**Owners:** card owners + panel
- Mark each card pass/fail with evidence links.

## Step 3 — Enforce fail actions
**Owners:** cross-functional panel
- Apply automatic hold rules for failed critical cards.

## Step 4 — Publish card status memo
**Owners:** HR + Legal + Payroll
- Share daily pass/fail board and blockers.

## Step 5 — Post-case card audit
**Owners:** Compliance + operations lead
- Analyze most frequent failed cards and improve controls.

## Required document checklist
- Employee profile and contract context
- Route rationale memo + fallback route
- City-level interpretation memo
- Evidence chronology ledger
- Script package and briefing records
- Payroll/severance/IIT/social worksheet
- Settlement and acknowledgment records
- Payment proof artifacts
- Decision-card status log
- Closure memo and archive index

## Exception handling playbook
- **Failed card disputed:** escalate with evidence-based tie-break note.
- **Owner unavailable:** activate backup owner and preserve SLA.
- **New fact invalidates passed card:** reopen card and rollback state.
- **Deadline override request:** document unresolved failed cards and formal risk acceptance.
- **Repeat failures on same card:** trigger template/workflow redesign.

## System implementation notes
- Add card-state fields (pass/fail/open) in workflow.
- Require evidence attachments for pass status.
- Auto-block downstream steps on critical card failure.
- Dashboard KPIs: fail frequency by card, time-to-pass, dispute linkage.
- Monthly review to recalibrate card definitions.

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** passing cards without proof.  
  **Prevention:** mandatory evidence links for every pass.
- **Mistake:** ignoring local card in “standard” cases.  
  **Prevention:** make Card 2 mandatory.
- **Mistake:** payroll card checked too late.  
  **Prevention:** Card 4 before communication scheduling.
- **Mistake:** communication card delegated informally.  
  **Prevention:** named spokesperson and briefing logs.
- **Mistake:** weak closure card enforcement.  
  **Prevention:** retrieval test requirement.

## FAQ

### 1) Why decision cards instead of long SOP text?
Cards improve real-time decision speed and consistency.

### 2) Can one failed card block a case?
Yes, for critical cards (route, evidence, payroll, local interpretation).

### 3) How often should cards be reviewed?
Daily for active cases and after any material update.

### 4) Who owns final go/hold?
Cross-functional HR, legal, and payroll leadership.

### 5) Is local interpretation always required?
Yes, because city-level practice can materially affect defensibility.

### 6) What is the most common avoidable failure?
Proceeding while payroll or evidence cards are still unresolved.

### 7) How does this improve over time?
By tracking recurring failed cards and hardening controls.
