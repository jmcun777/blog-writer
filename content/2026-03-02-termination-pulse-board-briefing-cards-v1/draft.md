# China Employee Termination: Board Briefing Cards for Foreign Employers (Hourly Testing Run)

## Key takeaways
- Foreign employers get better outcomes when China termination decisions are made through short, disciplined briefing cards instead of long narrative memos.
- Route legality is only Card 1; Cards 2-5 (evidence, communication, settlement, escalation) determine execution resilience.
- Pulse-style card stacks help non-China leaders decide quickly while preserving legal and payroll discipline.
- Most preventable losses come from cross-functional drift, not from inability to find a legal route.
- Keep legal depth in appendix and use the cards as the primary decision narrative.

## Pulse lead
This run converts the topic into a **board briefing card stack**. The aim is practical: give foreign executives a compact decision package they can use in one meeting. Each card answers one high-impact question and ends with an explicit action rule.

## Plain-language legal context first
China’s Labor Contract Law defines the legal boundaries for termination and severance obligations. State Council implementation regulations and SPC labor-dispute interpretation guide how disputes assess evidence quality, process fairness, and procedural coherence. For foreign clients, the practical translation is simple: route legality, records, communication behavior, and payout execution must stay aligned from pre-action approval to final closure.

## Card 1 — Route viability (legal baseline)
**Question:** Is the selected route legally available for this fact pattern?

**Readout:** Treat route availability as conditional. The team should state route prerequisites and what would invalidate them. Avoid approving route labels without route conditions.

**Action rule:** Approve only when route + conditions + owner accountability are all explicit.

## Card 2 — Evidence confidence (defensibility baseline)
**Question:** If challenged, where is the weakest evidentiary point?

**Readout:** Many files are document-heavy but chronology-light. A complete chronology index with owner sign-off is more useful than a large unstructured record set.

**Action rule:** No employee-facing step until evidence gaps are either remediated or formally accepted with written risk rationale.

## Card 3 — Communication discipline (manager risk control)
**Question:** Are HR and manager messages aligned to the approved route and settlement logic?

**Readout:** Script drift is a recurring source of avoidable exposure. Communication should be handled as a governed control, not a personal style issue.

**Action rule:** One final script version, one manager briefing checkpoint, one controlled post-meeting record.

## Card 4 — Settlement and payroll coherence (execution integrity)
**Question:** Can payroll and legal explain every settlement component clearly, consistently, and on schedule?

**Readout:** Settlement disputes often begin when calculation logic and communication logic diverge. Itemization and timing clarity are critical for trust and closure quality.

**Action rule:** Freeze external commitments until settlement worksheet, payment timeline, and tax assumptions are jointly validated.

## Card 5 — Escalation authority (edge-case governance)
**Question:** If a high-risk issue appears near execution, who can pause, reroute, or proceed?

**Readout:** Decision paralysis near cutover creates rushed actions. Teams need named primary and backup decision owners.

**Action rule:** No go-live without explicit escalation authority and fallback route documented.

## Card 6 — Local calibration (global-local balance)
**Question:** Does one global process require city-level overlays?

**Readout:** National legal anchors are stable, but local handling patterns can vary in practice. Central consistency should coexist with local calibration.

**Action rule:** Preserve global control baseline; add city-specific escalation overlays where variance is material.

## Card 7 — Closure integrity (true endpoint)
**Question:** What defines “closed” for this case?

**Readout:** Signature is a milestone, not the endpoint. Closure quality requires payment evidence, archive completeness, and control checks.

**Action rule:** Case closes only when signature + payment proof + archive verification are complete.

## Practical implications for foreign employers
- **HQ boards:** Review card stack status instead of long legal prose for faster comparable decisions.
- **Regional HR:** Convert card outputs into go/no-go gates with owners and SLAs.
- **Payroll/finance:** co-own Card 4 outcomes and communicate constraints early.
- **Local managers:** treat Card 3 as mandatory compliance behavior.

## Scenario snapshots
### Scenario A: Card 2 failed, case paused early
Route looked available, but evidence confidence was weak due to chronology mismatch. Team paused, remediated, and reran card stack. Outcome: slower execution, stronger defensibility.

### Scenario B: Card 4 flagged payout ambiguity
Settlement text was agreed but payroll itemization did not align with explanation language. Team relocked worksheet before communication. Outcome: reduced post-signature friction.

### Scenario C: Card 6 identified city variance
Single global process created risk in one city with different dispute sensitivity. Team added local escalation overlay while retaining baseline controls. Outcome: consistency with practical fit.

## Daily operations SOP (card-stack workflow)
1. Open case and generate Card 1-7 baseline.
2. Assign owners and deadlines for each non-green card.
3. Run legal/payroll parallel review for Cards 2 and 4.
4. Complete manager briefing and script lock for Card 3.
5. Run go/no-go with unresolved-card thresholds.
6. Execute with exception logging and change-control.
7. Close only when Card 7 criteria are met.

## Required document checklist
- Route memo + route condition table
- Evidence chronology index + gap log
- Communication script package + manager briefing proof
- Settlement worksheet + tax treatment note
- Escalation authority matrix
- Card-stack status sheet with owners
- Meeting and exception records
- Payment proof and reconciliation package
- Closure completeness report

## Exception handling playbook
- **Card 2 red at cutoff:** auto-hold and remediation escalation.
- **Card 3 drift event:** issue corrected summary, legal review note, and retraining step.
- **Card 4 changes late:** relock settlement file and rerun approvals.
- **Card 5 owner unavailable:** activate backup authority before further action.
- **Card 6 local mismatch:** add city overlay and adjust risk threshold.

## System implementation notes
Suggested system fields:
- card ID/status (1-7)
- owner, SLA, and evidence link
- route condition status
- script version and briefing timestamp
- settlement lock version and payroll verifier
- escalation decision log
- closure completeness score

Control recommendations:
- hard-stop on unresolved critical cards
- mandatory re-approval on route/script/settlement changes
- immutable version history for scripts and worksheets
- weekly dashboard of repeated red-card patterns

## Common mistakes and prevention
- **Mistake:** Route approved without full card stack.  
  **Prevention:** card-stack completion required pre-action.
- **Mistake:** Payroll enters late.  
  **Prevention:** parallel legal-payroll review by default.
- **Mistake:** Script treated as optional guidance.  
  **Prevention:** communication control as compliance gate.
- **Mistake:** closure declared too early.  
  **Prevention:** enforce Card 7 end-state checks.

## FAQ
### Is the board-card model legally required?
No. It is a governance format to improve consistency and decision quality.

### Can smaller teams use it?
Yes. Start with Cards 1-5 and add 6-7 as complexity grows.

### Why use cards instead of long memos?
Cards improve cross-functional clarity, speed, and accountability.

### Is mutual separation always safer?
Often lower-friction, but still dependent on documentation and payout control quality.

### When should external PRC counsel be mandatory?
High-risk unilateral routes, protected-status concerns, restructuring, and multi-city complexity.
