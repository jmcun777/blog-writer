# China Termination Payroll and Severance: Daily Execution SOP for Foreign Employers in 2026

## Key takeaways
- The biggest foreign-employer risk in China exits is not usually the formula itself; it is poor process control between HR, payroll, and manager approvals.
- Treat each termination as a **single payroll-controlled case** with a legal basis record, payment map, tax treatment note, and evidence archive.
- Build timeline discipline: pre-close checks at D-7, locked approvals by D-3, payroll-day reconciliation, and post-run statutory offboarding verification.
- Use exception playbooks in advance for missing handover documents, disputed termination grounds, and late severance approvals.
- Where local implementation differs (especially IIT/social insurance handling details), record uncertainty and obtain city-level confirmation before payroll cut-off.

## Why this topic matters now for foreign employers
For foreign employers operating in China through an entity or EOR structure, employee exits are one of the highest-cost payroll moments. A normal monthly payroll error can usually be corrected in the next run; a termination payroll error often becomes a dispute file, a delayed release process, or an avoidable settlement negotiation. In practice, this is where finance, HR, and line management either demonstrate operational maturity or generate preventable risk.

The law-level framework is relatively clear on core obligations, but daily execution is where teams struggle: unclear ownership of severance calculations, incomplete evidence trails for termination grounds, and weak handoff controls between HR operations and payroll processing. For foreign employers that need predictable compliance, the decision is straightforward: build a repeatable termination payroll SOP now, not after a disputed case appears.

## Regulatory baseline (concise): what is confirmed vs. where interpretation risk remains
At a high level, two legal anchors matter most for payroll operations tied to termination. The **Labor Contract Law** sets the framework for lawful termination and severance exposure. The **Social Insurance Law** establishes employer obligations around social insurance participation and related administration, which affects offboarding timing and final-pay controls. Tax treatment sits in a separate framework and should be handled using current tax administration practice and local rules reflected in payroll systems.

What sources broadly agree on is that termination is not a “manager-only” decision; it creates statutory and payroll obligations that must be executed with evidence and timing discipline. Where risk remains is implementation detail: city-level bureau practices, local filing windows, and treatment logic for certain final-payment components can differ in real operations. Employers should therefore classify any non-standard item as “requires local confirmation” before releasing the final run.

## What a foreign employer should control first: the risk map
Instead of starting from policy commentary, start from failure points seen in daily payroll execution:

1. **Wrong legal basis code in internal records** leads to the wrong severance assumption and bad manager communication.
2. **Unapproved variable components** (unused leave payout, incentive treatment, clawback offsets) enter payroll too late.
3. **IIT treatment decisions are made ad hoc** without a retained calculation note, creating rework during employee queries.
4. **Social insurance/housing fund offboarding timing is inconsistent** with payroll cut-off, causing contribution mismatches.
5. **No evidence archive** means even correct calculations become hard to defend in labor dispute channels.

For an overseas management team, this risk map is the practical reason to invest in an SOP: it reduces legal escalation probability and gives finance predictable close quality.

## Scenario-led examples (how problems usually appear)
### Scenario 1: Mutual termination agreed, but severance approval arrives after payroll lock
HR confirms a mutual termination date and drafts an agreement, but business leadership negotiates commercial terms late. Payroll closes on schedule with regular salary only, while severance is pushed to an off-cycle payment. The employee receives mixed messages, asks for tax clarification, and disputes payment timing. The issue was not legal theory; it was timeline governance.

**Operational lesson:** define a D-3 hard gate: no signed approval package, no in-cycle severance; and communicate this in writing to manager and employee representative before payroll lock.

### Scenario 2: Performance-related termination attempted without complete evidence package
A line manager requests termination for performance reasons and expects immediate payroll finalization. HR discovers missing review records and inconsistent warning documentation. Termination basis becomes disputed, and payroll cannot classify the payment components confidently. The team then uses a temporary settlement structure under time pressure.

**Operational lesson:** require an HR legal-basis checklist at D-7 and classify cases as standard/high-risk before payroll touches the payment logic.

## Daily operations SOP (role-by-role)
### SOP stage A: Case intake and classification (D-10 to D-7)
**HR owner**
1. Open a termination case ID in HRIS/payroll workflow.
2. Record proposed termination date, legal basis type, and contract facts (start date, tenure, role, city).
3. Run legal-basis completeness check (documents present/missing).
4. Assign risk tier: standard, controlled exception, or high-risk dispute-prone.

**Manager owner**
1. Submit business rationale and timeline constraints.
2. Confirm handover obligations and asset return requirements.
3. Nominate one business approver for payment package sign-off.

**Payroll owner**
1. Freeze employee variable-pay intake pending HR classification.
2. Pre-map final-pay components: salary through end date, unused leave handling, statutory/contractual severance, adjustments.
3. Create preliminary IIT treatment worksheet with “assumption” tags.

### SOP stage B: Calculation and approval control (D-7 to D-3)
**HR + Payroll joint control**
1. Convert legal basis into payment rules using approved internal matrix.
2. Calculate severance and final-pay components in a version-controlled worksheet.
3. Attach source assumptions (contract clause, policy reference, calculation method, local rule note).
4. Route for dual approval: HR compliance lead + payroll lead.

**Finance/control owner**
1. Validate funding and cost-center mapping.
2. Confirm whether payment is in regular run or approved off-cycle.
3. Lock journal mapping before payroll close.

**SLA standard**
- D-7: case complete enough for first calculation
- D-5: draft package sent for approvals
- D-3: approvals locked, employee communication finalized

### SOP stage C: Payroll execution day (D-0)
**Payroll owner**
1. Execute final-pay run using approved package version only.
2. Validate IIT treatment and net-pay output against reviewed worksheet.
3. Reconcile gross-to-net and contribution fields for anomalies.
4. Store output files in case archive with timestamp.

**HR owner**
1. Deliver final settlement statement to employee.
2. Confirm handover completion status and certificates documentation.
3. Log any dispute notice or employee objection immediately.

### SOP stage D: Post-run closure (D+1 to D+7)
**Payroll + HR owner**
1. Verify statutory offboarding actions submitted/processed.
2. Confirm social insurance/housing fund status transitions in local process.
3. Close case only after evidence packet is complete and indexed.
4. Issue post-case review note for any exception events.

## Exception handling playbook (pre-written responses)
### Exception A: Missing documents before D-3
- **Rule:** no complete package, no final severance in regular run.
- **Action:** pay uncontested components only (if compliant and approved), issue formal notice of pending items, schedule approved off-cycle payment once documentation is complete.
- **Control:** escalation to HR compliance lead within same business day.

### Exception B: Disputed payment treatment raised by employee
- **Rule:** freeze contested component changes unless approved by HR compliance and payroll lead.
- **Action:** provide calculation note, legal-basis summary, and review path; keep communication centralized through HR case owner.
- **Control:** maintain a dispute log with timestamped responses.

### Exception C: Late manager approval after payroll lock
- **Rule:** no manual override into locked payroll without emergency approval chain.
- **Action:** move to off-cycle with explicit tax and contribution review.
- **Control:** track as SLA breach and include in monthly governance metrics.

### Exception D: Local bureau/system uncertainty on treatment logic
- **Rule:** classify as “interpretation risk” and avoid definitive language.
- **Action:** obtain city-level confirmation through advisor/vendor channel; document response and apply prospectively.
- **Control:** add note to local payroll rulebook and train case owners.

## System implementation notes (fields, controls, audit trail)
A practical China termination payroll workflow works best when configuration enforces behavior, not just policy PDFs.

**Core data fields to require**
- Termination case ID (unique)
- City/location code (for local rule mapping)
- Legal basis category (controlled picklist)
- Termination effective date
- Service start date and tenure basis date
- Severance months/amount calculation fields
- Final salary period fields
- Leave payout fields
- IIT treatment code and worksheet attachment link
- Social insurance/housing fund status flag
- Approval status and approver IDs

**Controls to implement**
- Hard-stop rule: no final run if approval state is incomplete.
- Version control on calculation sheets; only latest approved version can post.
- Automated exception flags: negative net pay, unusual tax ratio, missing tenure field, unclosed offboarding status.
- Evidence archive integration: each case stores documents, payroll output, and communication log.

**Audit log minimum**
- Who changed payment components and when
- Which version of worksheet was used for payment
- Who approved legal basis and commercial terms
- When employee communication was sent
- When statutory offboarding confirmations were logged

## Common mistakes and how to prevent them
### Mistake 1: Treating termination as only an HR process
**Prevention:** run a joint HR-payroll workflow with shared ownership and SLA gates.

### Mistake 2: Running severance from spreadsheet memory, not controlled assumptions
**Prevention:** use standardized calculation templates with mandatory source note fields.

### Mistake 3: Overpromising certainty on tax treatment in edge cases
**Prevention:** use factual wording, document assumptions, and escalate local uncertainties before payout.

### Mistake 4: Delaying employee communication until after payment
**Prevention:** require pre-run communication pack approval by D-3.

### Mistake 5: Closing case before statutory offboarding proof is captured
**Prevention:** enforce D+7 closure checklist and prevent case closure without filing evidence.

## FAQ
### 1) Is severance in China just a payroll formula problem?
No. Formula accuracy matters, but most escalation risk comes from process defects: wrong basis classification, weak approvals, missing evidence, or poor communication timing.

### 2) Should termination payments always be processed in the regular payroll run?
Not always. In-cycle processing is preferred when approvals are complete by cut-off. If not, use a governed off-cycle process with explicit documentation and review.

### 3) Can foreign employers rely on one national process for every China city?
Use a common control framework nationally, but keep local implementation notes. City-level handling details can differ, especially in administration timing and supporting document expectations.

### 4) What is the minimum evidence set to defend a termination payroll outcome?
At minimum: legal basis record, contract and tenure proof, versioned calculation worksheet, approval trail, payment output, and employee communication history.

### 5) How should teams handle uncertain tax treatment for a final-payment component?
Do not guess. Mark the item as uncertain, obtain local confirmation through your advisor/vendor channel, and keep an auditable treatment note before payment release.

### 6) What KPI best predicts future termination disputes?
A strong leading indicator is the share of cases missing D-3 approval lock. High late-approval rates usually correlate with post-payroll disputes and off-cycle corrections.
